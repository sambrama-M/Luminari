Sanchi Stupa: A Timeless Dome of Peace and Enlightenment!

About SANCHI STUPA

The Sanchi Stupa, located near Bhopal in Madhya Pradesh, is one of the most iconic examples of Buddhist architecture in India. It was commissioned by Emperor Ashoka in the 3rd century BCE to enshrine the relics of Gautama Buddha. This stupa is not just a structure but a symbol of enlightenment and peace, deeply rooted in Buddhist philosophy. The stupa itself has a hemispherical dome, which represents the dharma (cosmic law and order) and the universe. At its core lies a relic chamber, believed to house the remains of Buddha or objects associated with him. Surrounding the stupa is a circular pathway, where devotees perform circumambulation (pradakshina) as an act of reverence and devotion. One of the most fascinating aspects of the Sanchi Stupa is its four magnificent toranas (gateways), which face the cardinal directions—north, south, east, and west. These gateways are masterpieces of ancient Indian sculpture, intricately carved with scenes from the Jataka tales and the life of Buddha. The carvings depict Buddha in aniconic forms—such as a bodhi tree, a wheel of dharma (Dharmachakra), or an empty throne—reflecting the Buddhist idea of non-representation. The Ashokan pillar near the stupa is another noteworthy feature. Although only the lower shaft remains at the site, the capital with four lions is preserved in a museum and is similar to the one at Sarnath, which is India’s national emblem. The Sanchi Stupa is also surrounded by smaller stupas and monastic structures, reflecting the spread of Buddhism over centuries. It underwent expansions during the Shunga dynasty, who added the decorative stone balustrades and toranas, enhancing its artistic appeal. In 1989, the Sanchi Stupa was declared a UNESCO World Heritage Site, recognizing its cultural and historical importance. Today, it stands as a testament to the enduring legacy of Buddhism, attracting pilgrims, historians, and art lovers from across the globe.
