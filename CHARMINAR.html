About the Charminar

The Charminar, an iconic monument in Hyderabad, Telangana, was built in 1591 by Sultan Muhammad Quli Qutb Shah to mark the founding of the city and commemorate the end of a devastating plague. Standing 56 meters tall, it features four grand arches representing the pillars of Islam: faith, prayer, charity, and fasting. The structure is a blend of Islamic, Persian, Mughal, and Hindu architectural styles, with intricate stucco work, floral patterns, and calligraphic inscriptions. Its towering minarets, rising to 48 meters, give the monument its name, "Charminar," meaning "four minarets." Once a thriving trade hub, the area surrounding the Charminar, including the famous Laad Bazaar, remains a center for local crafts, jewelry, and street food. The monument continues to be a cultural and historical symbol of Hyderabad, attracting millions of visitors each year and hosting significant events like the Ramzan celebrations and the Deccan Festival.

It also serves as a reminder of the city's rich historical past and its evolving cultural landscape. As one of India's most recognizable landmarks, the Charminar remains a beacon of Hyderabad's heritage and continues to play a central role in the community's identity.
